# Life Pilot Core Testing Partner Pack

Last updated: 2026-05-30

## Purpose

This pack is for prospective core testing partners.

It is designed for a Morella-style, Narawindi-style, NFP, social enterprise, community, place-based, employment, wellbeing, or support-sector partner who may help test Life Pilot with real users and real support contexts.

This is not a public sales deck and not a finished implementation agreement.

It is a practical testing-partnership pack.

## One-line positioning

Life Pilot helps people bring the scattered parts of real life into view, see what needs attention, and take the next practical step.

## Core line

> For when life throws a whole lot of life at you.

## Supporting promise

> See what matters. Plan what's next. Take the next practical step.

## Why a testing partner is needed

Life Pilot is being built from lived experience and practical use, not abstract productivity theory.

A core testing partner helps validate whether the product works in real-world conditions:

- People have scattered information.
- Pressure builds across money, work, home, documents, appointments, and support.
- Support is often fragmented.
- Users may be dealing with irregular income, arrears, job search, obligations, reporting dates, or household admin.
- People need practical next steps, not vague motivation.

The purpose of testing is to learn what is useful, safe, clear, and practical before scaling.

## What Life Pilot is

Life Pilot is a personal life-navigation system.

It helps users bring together:

- Money
- Work
- Home admin
- Documents
- People and support
- Identity-safe details
- Obligations
- Income changes
- Reporting dates
- Decisions

Then it helps them see what matters, plan what could happen next, and take the next practical step.

## What Life Pilot is not

Life Pilot is not:

- A generic productivity app.
- A case-management platform.
- A welfare intake system.
- A financial advice tool.
- A crisis intervention service.
- A replacement for professional support.
- A surveillance tool for partners.
- A place to force users into shame-based compliance.

## Current product areas

### Life Admin

Tasks, calendar, documents, home routines, appointments, obligations, and everyday admin.

### Financial Life

Income, bills, arrears, safe-to-spend, budget visibility, reporting dates, BNPL/stored value, and financial pressure signals.

### Future Life

Scenario planning, pressure weeks, income drops, bill shock, arrears catch-up, option comparison, and next practical steps.

### People & Support Network

Chosen family, trusted contacts, household members, support workers, recruiters, providers, communication preferences, pronouns, and identity-safe relationship records.

## Companion app context

### Job Pilot

A companion app for job search, job capture, applications, tasks, evidence, reporting, and next steps.

### Gig Pilot

A companion app for irregular income, gig work, platform/source tracking, costs, earnings evidence, and future Life Pilot integration.

Gig Pilot is platform/source agnostic. It must not become DoorDash-only, delivery-only, or hustle-culture.

## Social-impact overlay context

real Life Pilot is the social-impact overlay for Life Pilot.

Positioning:

> Support for when life gets very real.

The testing partner may help validate future support workflows, but inclusion is part of core Life Pilot, not only the overlay.

Hard rule:

No token modules. Every social-impact feature must either change the app experience in a culturally safe or identity-safe way, or help the user complete a practical action.

## What the partner helps test

A core testing partner helps test:

1. Whether users understand the Life Pilot concept.
2. Whether setup feels manageable.
3. Which life areas matter most.
4. Which pressure signals are useful.
5. Which language feels respectful and non-shaming.
6. Whether Future Life scenarios help users compare options.
7. Whether users trust support-sharing controls.
8. What support workers or trusted contacts need to see.
9. What should never be shared by default.
10. Whether Life Pilot helps users complete practical actions.

## What participants may do in a test

Depending on the test design, participants may:

- Create a simple Life Pilot profile.
- Add sample or real life-admin items.
- Add bills, obligations, or reporting dates.
- Add job/gig/work-related activity if relevant.
- Add trusted contacts or support preferences.
- Review dashboard pressure signals.
- Try a Future Life scenario.
- Identify one next practical step.
- Review what would or would not be safe to share.
- Give feedback on wording, trust, usefulness, and friction.

## What the partner may do

A partner may:

- Help recruit a small testing cohort.
- Provide practical context about user needs.
- Help review language and safety controls.
- Help define consent and support boundaries.
- Observe aggregate themes where appropriate.
- Help assess whether the product is useful in a real service context.

The partner should not receive individual user information without clear consent and safe export/sharing controls.

## What is not being tested yet

Unless explicitly agreed, the core partner test should not test:

- Full social-impact case workflows.
- Clinical support workflows.
- Legal or financial advice.
- Automated eligibility decisions.
- Production integrations.
- Large-scale partner dashboards.
- High-risk data sharing.
- Broad database/schema changes.

## Safety and consent principles

Testing must be based on:

- Clear participant consent.
- Plain-English explanation of what is being tested.
- No surprise sharing.
- User-controlled export/sharing where possible.
- Safe display names where relevant.
- Separation of legal name and chosen/safe display name where needed.
- Optional pronouns.
- Chosen family and trusted contacts.
- Non-shaming language.
- No assumptions about family, gender, income stability, household type, work capacity, or support needs.

## Evidence to collect

Testing should collect evidence about:

- What users understand quickly.
- Where users get stuck.
- Which areas they actually use.
- Which terms need changing.
- Whether dashboard signals help or overwhelm.
- Whether Future Life scenarios support decisions.
- Whether users can identify a next practical step.
- Whether users would return to the product.
- Whether the partner can see a safe, practical role.

## Possible success measures

Early success does not mean proving broad impact.

Early success means:

- Users understand the purpose.
- Users can add enough information to make the dashboard useful.
- Users can identify what needs attention.
- Users can choose a next practical step.
- Users do not feel shamed by the product language.
- Users understand what is private and what could be shared.
- Partners can see practical use without turning the product into surveillance or case management.

## Proposed test structure

### Stage 1: Partner alignment

- Review the Life Pilot purpose.
- Confirm test audience.
- Confirm safety boundaries.
- Confirm what will and will not be tested.

### Stage 2: Small guided test

- Small cohort.
- Supported onboarding.
- Feedback after first use.
- Capture setup friction and comprehension.

### Stage 3: Practical action test

- Users identify one real pressure point or admin issue.
- Users use Life Pilot to identify options.
- Users choose one next practical step.

### Stage 4: Partner review

- Review themes, not unsafe individual detail.
- Identify product improvements.
- Decide whether to continue, revise, or stop.

## Partner ask

The partner is being asked to help validate Life Pilot in a practical, safe, grounded way.

The ask may include:

- A small number of participants.
- A nominated partner contact.
- Feedback on product language.
- Feedback on support-sharing boundaries.
- Feedback on what would make Life Pilot genuinely useful in context.

## Brand rules for this pack

Use:

- Warm, grounded, practical language.
- Real Life Signal framing.
- Future Life language.
- Non-shaming support wording.
- Practical testing language.

Avoid:

- Charity/welfare clichés.
- Overclaiming impact.
- Saying the product prevents crisis.
- Calling users vulnerable as the main identity.
- Productivity-bro language.
- Cockpit, captain, control tower, pilot-wing, or airline language.
- Treating inclusion as a campaign layer.
